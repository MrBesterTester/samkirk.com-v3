Essay 1 — Why are you interested in the Fellows program?

Form prompt: "Why are you interested in participating in the Fellows program?" (1–2 paragraphs, required)

I've spent forty years as a test and diagnostic engineer — from standalone POST firmware for Class 5 CO telephone switches at TRW/Vidar in 1980, through 10 Gbps ethernet silicon verification on SPARC IV at Sun, to a LoRA-fine-tuned Llama 3.2 model for hardware diagnostics three years ago. What draws me to the Fellows program is not a career pivot but a lineage step. The discipline of asking "how does this system fail, and what failure modes have I not yet imagined?" is the discipline Nancy Leveson formalized in SAFEWARE (1995) and extended through STAMP and STPA — and it is the same discipline at the core of AI safety: red-teaming, evaluation, scalable oversight, reliability under distribution shift. The intellectual inheritance is explicit, even if the ML research community does not always make it visible.

The Fellows program is the right container for that inheritance to become a contribution. I'm not joining to build my first ML intuition; I'm joining to bring a structured systems-safety lens to a research team already doing the hard empirical work. The ML Systems & Performance workstream is the natural fit — it sits at the boundary where engineering rigor, model behavior, and operational reliability meet, and where my cross-stack instincts (silicon, firmware, cloud, now LLMs) are most useful. Four months of full-time research, paired with an Anthropic mentor, is the shortest path I know of from "forty years of diagnostic practice" to "a tangible AI safety research artifact."

Essay 2 — Research areas you're excited about?

Form prompt: "With your selected team(s) in mind, tell us briefly about one or more research areas you're excited about right now, and why." (1 paragraph, required)

One direction I'm eager to explore is applying Leveson's STPA (Systems-Theoretic Process Analysis) to language-model training and deployment. STPA treats a system as a control structure, enumerates unsafe control actions, and traces them to process-model mismatches and missing feedback — it produces a structured hazard taxonomy rather than a list of empirical test cases. Modern AI red-teaming is enormously powerful empirically but largely ad hoc in its taxonomy; STPA offers a method that complements empirical red-teaming by forcing the analyst to name the control loops a model participates in and reason about what the absence of a particular feedback signal would cause. I think this is especially relevant to ML Systems & Performance questions — deployment reliability, evaluation coverage, failure-mode cataloguing — and it's a method most ML researchers have not been exposed to. I have drafted a four-month research proposal for this work — anchored on four canonical Leveson texts (Engineering a Safer World, the STPA Handbook, the CAST Handbook, and her 2023 An Introduction to System Safety Engineering) and organized around a biweekly internal seminar that I would lead, culminating in a written report and a scoped STPA analysis of one Claude deployment case. Beyond STPA specifically, I'm interested in any line of work that treats evaluation as a structured engineering practice rather than a benchmark exercise.

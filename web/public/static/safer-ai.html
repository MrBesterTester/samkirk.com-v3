1. Motivation & Honest Positioning

Modern AI safety at frontier labs is enormously powerful empirically — red-teaming, evaluations, Constitutional AI, RLHF, interpretability — but its hazard taxonomy is largely built from the bottom up: observed failure patterns aggregated into categories. This is exactly the practice Leveson documented in aerospace and medical device safety before STAMP: useful but brittle, hard to reason about completeness, and vulnerable to missing hazards that don't resemble past failures.

Leveson's contribution across four decades has been a complementary top-down method: treat any safety-critical system as a control structure, enumerate the unsafe control actions it can produce, and trace each to the underlying process-model mismatches and missing feedback loops that would cause it. The result is a structured hazard catalog derived from system architecture, not from incident history. This is the method that now underwrites aviation, nuclear, automotive, and medical-device safety-critical work.

Anthropic ships at startup velocity. This proposal matches that cadence rather than the contemplative graduate-seminar shape Leveson's MIT course uses. Every phase produces a concrete artifact, every weekly working session ends with a named artifact and a scope locked for the next session, and the four-month deliverable is something Anthropic can pick up and extend Monday morning — not a monograph that gets archived and forgotten.

The research claim. Leveson's framework can be adapted to the deployed-LLM context — treating a Claude model plus its deployment surround (prompt format, tools, retrieval, human oversight, training pipeline) as a control structure — to produce a structured hazard taxonomy that complements Anthropic's existing empirical safety practice. The method is not a replacement for red-teaming; it's a scaffolding that tells red-teamers where to look by naming the control loops a model participates in and reasoning about the failure modes each loop can exhibit.
Honest positioning. Sam Kirk has forty years of test and diagnostic engineering (TRW/Vidar, IBM/ROLM, Sun Microsystems, Teradyne, Cadence, Talis Biomedical, SAK Consulting), which is the practitioner lineage that system safety engineering formalizes. He has not previously practiced STAMP/STPA/CAST methods themselves. This proposal is scoped to what a full-time Fellow with his background can credibly produce in 4 months: a rigorous literature engagement, a structured first-pass application to one concrete Claude deployment context, and a seminar-led knowledge transfer inside Anthropic.

How the 2012 and 2023 books relate (and why both are in the corpus)

The two MIT Press monographs in the corpus can look redundant at first glance — same author, overlapping vocabulary — but Leveson is explicit in the 2012 preface that they are complementary, not substitutes. Engineering a Safer World (2012) is the manifesto-and-method book that introduces STAMP, STPA, and CAST as a connected framework. An Introduction to System Safety Engineering (2023) is the literal update to SAFEWARE (1995) — a comprehensive textbook covering the whole System Safety field as it stands now, with STAMP/STPA/CAST as one major part among history, legal/ethical context, and policy.

Why both are in the project corpus. The 2012 book is the foundational source for the STAMP method Sam will adapt to Claude — the place where STAMP, STPA, and CAST are first argued for and laid out, and the place to go for the full Friendly Fire and SUBSAFE worked examples. The 2023 textbook is the current vocabulary and broader context, including the legal, ethical, and policy framings that matter when adapting the framework to AI deployment. Reading them in order 2012 → 2023 (which Phase 1 does) gives Sam foundation argument first, then current integrated reference.

3. Research Hypothesis & Questions

Primary hypothesis: STPA, applied to a deployed Claude model plus its deployment surround, produces a structured hazard taxonomy that (a) identifies hazards the current empirical red-teaming pipeline has not explicitly catalogued, and (b) gives Anthropic safety researchers a principled vocabulary for reasoning about coverage gaps in existing evaluations.

Research questions:

  1. Control-structure translation. What is the right STPA control-structure diagram for a deployed LLM? Where are the natural control boundaries (model vs. system prompt vs. tool use vs. human oversight vs. training pipeline)?
  2. Unsafe control actions for LLMs. How does the STPA vocabulary of "control action provided when it should not be," "not provided when it should be," "provided too early/too late," and "stopped too soon/applied too long" map onto LLM behavior (tool invocation, content refusal/compliance, tool parameter generation)?
  3. Process-model mismatch. What role does the model's internal representation of the user, the conversation, and the world play as a "process model" in STPA terms — and where can mismatch between that and reality produce unsafe control actions?
  4. Coverage comparison. How does an STPA-derived hazard list compare to Anthropic's empirical red-teaming taxonomy (from published model cards, RSP documents, and red-teaming posts)? Where do the two agree, and where does each surface hazards the other misses?
  5. CAST retrospective. Can CAST reconstruct a published AI incident (e.g., Bing Sydney 2023, a documented prompt-injection case) as a system-safety accident and extract lessons the empirical post-mortem did not emphasize?

5. Seminar Design — Weekly Working Sessions, Sam-Led

Anthropic ships at startup velocity, and this seminar matches that cadence rather than the contemplative graduate-seminar shape Leveson's MIT course uses. The seminar exists to drive shipping: STAMP/STPA/CAST gets applied to real Claude deployments week by week, and every session produces a concrete artifact that feeds the deliverables in Section 6. Sam leads; the assigned Anthropic mentor co-pilots when the topic intersects their work; attendees come with blockers and contribute live.

Pedagogical, not production. The artifacts shipped by the seminar are deliberately simple, worked teaching examples — not Anthropic's production STPA analysis, not its production hazard taxonomy, not its production eval suite. They exist so attendees can take the method and apply it to their own real-world, possibly confidential work after the Fellowship ends. Treat the seminar's output as basis material for downstream production work, not as a substitute for it.

Weekly session structure (60 min) — the rule of three over a biweekly cycle

The classical pedagogical pattern — "say what you're going to say, say it, then summarize what you said" — plays out across each biweekly cycle, not within a single session. The cycle pairs an odd week (present) with the following even week (review):

  • Odd weeks (W1, W3, W5, …, W15) — "Say what you'll do" + "Do it": Sam delivers slides on the week's concept, then states the deliverable (v1 of the worked example). The example is deliberately small — concept-first, not production-grade.
  • Even weeks (W2, W4, W6, …, W16) — "Summarize what you did": Sam reviews the just-posted v1 deliverable; the group discusses it and proposes improvements. Sam revises to v2 in the days that follow and posts both v1 and v2 to the seminar archive — see artifact lifecycle.
  • Between cycles: attendees read the assigned material in preparation for the next odd-week presentation. Reading is recommended for attendees, mandatory for Sam (he's preparing the slides).

Sam's weekly prep produces slides and deliverable as a single piece of work — the slides walk attendees through what the deliverable shows.

Odd-week session (60 min) — Present concepts & state the deliverable

  • 5 min — Status. What was posted to the seminar archive since last session (v2 of the previous cycle's deliverable). What's on the table today.
  • 20 min — Theory / concept. The Leveson concept under discussion this cycle (e.g., "unsafe control actions in a tool-use loop"), via slides.
  • 25 min — State the deliverable. Sam presents v1 of the worked example: a simple, clear, useful demonstration of the concept applied to a Claude scenario. Attendees can pair-program here if it's a build session (see attendee mechanisms above).
  • 10 min — Q&A and reading guide. Quick clarifying questions; reading assignment for next cycle posted in the shared notes.

Even-week session (60 min) — Review & discuss the posted deliverable

  • 5 min — Recap. Quick recap of last week's posted v1 deliverable for anyone who missed the odd-week session.
  • 45 min — Critique & discussion. Attendees appraise v1 in detail — surface gaps, propose alternatives, push back where it doesn't land. Mentor weighs in. Sam takes notes; no defending in the moment. Possible improvements get logged in the shared notes.
  • 10 min — Decisions for v2. Sam confirms which of the proposed improvements land in v2; trade-offs are Sam's call (with mentor input on anything contested). Cycle wraps.
Demonstrate the concept in the simplest useful fashion. The deliverable each cycle is a worked example, not a polished production artifact. The bar is "this teaches the concept clearly enough that an Anthropic researcher can take it and apply the method to their own real-world work." Simpler is better. The two-pass artifact lifecycle (v1 → critique → v2) is what raises quality, not over-engineering on the first pass.

Artifact lifecycle: ship → appraise → revise → archive

Each cycle is one full lifecycle pass for the cycle's deliverable. The two-pass cycle (v1 → critique → v2) is structurally identical to the biweekly seminar cycle (odd-week present → even-week review):

StepWhat happens
Odd week — Ship v1 Sam presents concept slides and states v1 of the deliverable. Brief walkthrough; the artifact is on the wall, not just in slides. v1 is posted to the seminar archive immediately after the session.
Even week — Appraise Critique session. Attendees examine v1 in detail, surface gaps, propose alternatives, push back where it doesn't land. Mentor weighs in. Sam takes notes; no defending in the moment.
Between cycles — Revise Sam produces v2 incorporating the appraisal in the days between the even-week session and the next odd-week presentation. Scope of revision is constrained by the next cycle's prep — not every comment lands in v2; trade-offs are Sam's call (with mentor input on anything contested).
Archive — Post v1 + v2 Both versions live in the internal Anthropic seminar archive (URL/space TBD with mentor at kickoff). v1 is preserved alongside v2 so the revision history is part of the teaching value — attendees see the method improving through critique, not just the final answer.
Cycle reality. The two-pass cycle is the default for the eight cycles in the session plan. Small in-flight refinements (a glossary update, a quick UCA addition) can happen mid-cycle without going through the full lifecycle. Sam decides at the odd-week session which mode the cycle's artifact is in.

Format & logistics

  • Venue: Berkeley workspace (hybrid-friendly) with remote attendance supported; tracked on an internal Anthropic calendar/channel. Same time, same day, every week.
  • Attendance: Voluntary; targeted invites to ML Systems & Performance, Safety & Alignment, and Security & Red Team teams. The bar for attendance is "you'll contribute or learn something useful in 60 minutes" — not "you should keep up with the seminar."
  • Materials: Slide deck per session is the default — Sam prepares slides and the week's worked-example deliverable as a single piece of work. Slides walk attendees through what the deliverable shows; deliverable artifacts (diagrams, matrices, code, prose) are linked from the slides and posted to the seminar archive. Working notes in a shared doc capture the recap-discussion decisions inline.
  • Feedback loop: Each session's working-block output and decisions feed directly into the next week's draft. The seminar is the research process, not a separate communication channel about it.

6. Deliverables & Success Criteria

Deliverables

  1. Final research report — a written artifact integrating the Leveson framework with Anthropic's safety practice. Target 30–50 pages. Intended audience: internal Anthropic safety researchers, with an optional external-release version if Anthropic approves.
  2. Claude deployment STPA analysis — a structured hazard taxonomy for one scoped Claude deployment case (tool-use loop, agentic coding, or long-horizon planning), produced using the STPA method.
  3. CAST retrospective — a worked accident-analysis write-up of one published AI incident.
  4. Hazard-mapping matrix — a concrete artifact that maps STPA-derived hazards to Anthropic's existing empirical red-teaming categories, highlighting coverage overlaps and gaps.
  5. Seminar-series archive — eight slide decks + shared notes, suitable for onboarding future Anthropic researchers who want to learn STAMP/STPA/CAST quickly.
  6. Public companion write-up — a distilled, non-confidential version of the framework and insights, published on samkirk.com after Anthropic approval.
  7. STPA-guided eval prototype (minimum viable) — a small, scoped software prototype that takes one STPA-derived "unsafe control action" from the Phase 3 case study and generates targeted evaluation prompts. The artifact is the proof-of-concept that STPA can guide where red-teamers look — the central claim of the proposal. Built in Phase 4 alongside the final report, in Python or TypeScript depending on the Anthropic mentor's preferred stack. Open-source under MIT license if Anthropic approves; otherwise internal-only.

Success criteria

  • Minimum: Sam emerges from the Fellowship with practitioner-level fluency in STAMP/STPA/CAST and a peer-reviewed (internally, via the seminar) Claude-specific STPA analysis artifact.
  • Target: At least one STPA-derived hazard that prompts a concrete change to an Anthropic evaluation, red-teaming protocol, or safety documentation practice.
  • Stretch: A credible case for adopting STPA as a standing complement to empirical red-teaming, with a transition plan that doesn't require Sam's continued presence.

13. Alternative Prototype Scopes

Deliverable #7 commits to one specific prototype scope — the STPA-guided eval prototype. Two alternative scopes were considered and are documented here so the choice is on the record. If the assigned mentor and Anthropic team would prefer one of these alternatives at project kickoff, the swap is straightforward — Phase 4's deliverable changes; the methodology and report do not.

Alternative A — Hazard-taxonomy diff tool

What: A Python notebook (or small CLI) that takes the STPA-derived hazard list from Phase 3 and the published Anthropic empirical red-teaming taxonomy (from model cards, RSP documents, blog posts) and produces a structured diff — what's covered by both, what's only in STPA, what's only in the empirical taxonomy.

Why: Directly automates Research Question 4 (Coverage Comparison). Less ambitious than Deliverable #7 but produces a concrete, machine-checkable artifact that surfaces coverage gaps.

Trade-offs: Lighter weight to build (weekend project), more clearly bounded. Lacks the proof-of-concept punch — it documents differences rather than demonstrating that STPA can guide red-teaming. Right choice if the mentor prefers a low-risk, pure-analysis artifact.

Alternative B — Process-model probe

What: An instrumentation/logging harness that monitors a Claude tool-use loop in real time and flags potential process-model mismatches — places where the model's apparent internal representation of state diverges from the actual environment state.

Why: Directly tests the proposal's process-model-mismatch hypothesis (Research Question 3). Most ambitious of the three options; closest to a research instrument that Anthropic could deploy beyond the Fellowship.

Trade-offs: Genuine research-prototype territory — the operational definition of "process-model mismatch" in an LLM is itself an open question, so the prototype has to define what it's looking for as it goes. Risk of being half-finished by week 16. Probably needs co-development with the mentor and is the riskiest of the three. Right choice if the mentor wants to push into novel research territory and accepts the schedule risk.

Why Deliverable #7 chose the STPA-guided eval prototype

  • Most directly tests the proposal's central claim. The proposal argues STPA is a scaffolding that tells red-teamers where to look. The eval prototype is the smallest possible artifact that demonstrates this in practice — STPA hazard in, targeted eval prompt out.
  • Right scope for 4 months. One unsafe control action, one eval generator. Achievable as a side artifact alongside the report; not so ambitious as to threaten the report's completion.
  • Anthropic-relevant. Eval generation is an active research area at Anthropic; a Fellow producing a small new eval-generation tool fits naturally into existing workflows and is something Anthropic researchers can pick up and extend.
  • Sam can ship it. Daily Claude Code use, ~6 personal AI agents in production, and a portfolio of TypeScript/Python/Rust artifacts mean implementation risk is low. The risk is scope discipline, not capability — and Risks Section 8 explicitly addresses the discipline.

14. Frequently Asked Questions

Q1. How does this proposal build upon Sam's past experience as a test software engineer who now applies genAI to accelerate software development?

The proposal is calibrated to bridge exactly those two halves of Sam's profile, and it depends on both being present.

Test engineering is the practitioner lineage that Leveson formalized. Forty years of finding failure modes, edge cases, and reliability gaps — across hardware POST, online diagnostics, picosecond-accuracy ATE timing, 10 Gbps silicon verification on SPARC IV, and instrument-level test infrastructure at Talis Biomedical — is exactly the practitioner discipline that STAMP/STPA structures. Sam already has the instincts; the proposal supplies the formal vocabulary (control structure, unsafe control actions, process-model mismatch) that names what those instincts have been doing all along. This is the lineage step described explicitly in the Fellows essays — not a career pivot, a methodological upgrade.

Empirical AI red-teaming is the same craft, applied to LLM behavior. Modern red-teaming is the descendant of the failure-mode hunting Sam has practiced for decades, just translated to a different substrate. The proposal explicitly frames STPA as a complement to empirical red-teaming, not a replacement. Sam's testing background is what makes him able to evaluate where the empirical pipeline already covers a hazard and where it doesn't — without that ground truth, an STPA-only analysis would risk re-deriving things red-teamers already know by intuition, or missing the subset of hazards that empirical methods catch without naming.

GenAI development practice is what makes the Phase 3 case studies tractable. The proposal's three candidate Phase 3 scopes — tool-use loop, agentic coding, long-horizon planning — are not academic abstractions for Sam. He uses Claude Code daily on real engineering work, runs ~6 personal AI agents in production, built a LoRA-fine-tuned Llama 3.2 3B for hardware diagnostics end-to-end (samkirk.com/computer-diagnostics), and architected a Vertex AI RAG chatbot with source citations (samkirk.com/hire-me). That means Sam has firsthand observational data on the actual failure modes of LLM-driven coding agents — process-model mismatches, tool-invocation errors, context loss across long sessions, brittle handoffs in multi-agent workflows. Phase 3 anchors on deployments Sam already lives inside, not on hypothetical ones.

The seminar is the channel where both halves come together. Each biweekly session pairs a Leveson concept (test-engineering lineage) with a Claude-specific worked scenario (genAI-builder lineage). Anthropic researchers attending the seminar get something a hire from only one of those backgrounds cannot easily provide: an analyst who can speak STAMP/STPA vocabulary and ground every example in an LLM-driven workflow he has personally built, deployed, and debugged. The intellectual contribution to Anthropic is the conjunction of those two lineages — not either one alone.

The result for Anthropic. A Fellow whose first-language fluency is "what does this system actually do under stress, and what fails first" — applied with both classical structured-safety method and a builder's instinct for where modern LLM deployments break.

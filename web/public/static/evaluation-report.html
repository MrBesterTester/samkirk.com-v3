Fine-Tuned
Boundary scan testing is a method of testing that verifies the electrical behavior of a board at specific points, typically at the boundary between two sections of the board. The test uses a boundary scan interconnect (BSI) to capture the state of the board at the boundary, and then drives the BSI to the next boundary, capturing the state again. This process is repeated until all boundaries have been tested. The test is useful for detecting defects in the board's electrical behavior, such as open or short circuits, and for verifying that the board meets its functional specifications. Boundary scan testing is typically used in the following situations: (1) During the production test of a new board, to verify that it meets its functional specifications. (2) When a board is modified or repaired, to detect any changes to the board's electrical behavior. (3) When a board is being reworked or reprogrammed, to verify that the changes do not affect the board's electrical behavior. (4) When a board is being tested for compliance with a specific standard, such as the IEEE 1149.1 standard. The BSDL file is a text file that contains the boundary scan chain and the test pattern. The BSDL file is used to program the boundary scan interconnect on the board.

Ground Truth
Guided probe diagnostics uses pre-computed fault dictionaries to direct a technician's probe to the most diagnostic measurement points, minimizing the number of measurements needed to isolate a fault. The information-theoretic foundation is that each measurement should maximally reduce the entropy of the fault hypothesis space: H = -sum(P_i * log2(P_i)), where P_i is the probability of each remaining fault candidate. Step 1 — Build the fault dictionary. Using the board's digital netlist and a fault simulator (e.g., Mentor Tessent, Synopsys TetraMAX), inject stuck-at-0 and stuck-at-1 faults on every net. For each fault, simulate the board's functional test vectors and record the expected outputs at every observable node. Step 2 — Capture failing board signatures. Run the functional test on the failing board and record the actual outputs. This initial signature eliminates faults whose simulated responses do not match the observed failure pattern. Step 3 — Select the optimal probe point. The algorithm selects the point that maximizes information gain: I(probe) = H_before - E[H_after]. The ideal probe point splits remaining fault candidates into two equal groups (maximum entropy reduction = 1 bit per measurement). Step 4 — Measure and iterate. The technician probes the indicated point, reports the measured value, and the system eliminates inconsistent fault candidates. Repeat until only one candidate remains — typically requiring log2(N) measurements. Step 5 — Verify the diagnosed fault. Identify the specific net and fault type (stuck-at-0 or stuck-at-1). Perform physical inspection, replace or rework the defective element, and re-run the functional test to confirm.

Ground Truth
MLCCs and polymer capacitors have fundamentally different construction that determines their impedance characteristics in the critical 1-50 MHz decoupling range. An MLCC achieves capacitance through hundreds of thin ceramic dielectric layers (BaTiO3 for X7R, 1-5 um each) interleaved with electrodes. This yields very high capacitance density but with ESL of 0.3-1.0 nH. The self-resonant frequency is f_SRF = 1/(2*pi*sqrt(ESL*C)): a 10 uF 0805 MLCC with 0.7 nH ESL resonates at 1.9 MHz. Polymer capacitors use a polymer electrolyte providing lower ESR (5-15 milliohms) and higher capacitance (100-470 uF per part), but in larger packages with ESL of 2-5 nH. A 220 uF polymer with 3 nH ESL resonates at f_SRF = 196 kHz. The key tradeoff is anti-resonance between the two types. MLCCs additionally suffer from DC bias derating — an X7R 10 uF rated at 6.3V may lose 50-70% capacitance at 5V applied bias. For the 1-50 MHz range, the optimal strategy combines both: polymers for 100 kHz-2 MHz, 10 uF MLCCs for 2-10 MHz, 0.1 uF MLCCs for 10-50 MHz, with intermediate values to smooth anti-resonances.

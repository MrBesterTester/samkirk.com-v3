Concrete Everyday Examples of Category Diagrams

Real-world examples you can visualize and understand intuitively, with visual diagrams.

Example 1: TRIANGLE DIAGRAM

The Cooking Shortcut

The Story:

You're preparing ingredients for dinner. You need to chop onions.

Objects:
  • A = Whole onion (natural state)
  • B = Chopped onion (cut into pieces)
  • C = Finely minced onion (super small pieces)
The Paths:
  • f: Roughly chop the onion (A → B)
  • h: Fine mince the whole onion directly (A → C)
  • g: Further refine the chopped onion into mince (B → C)
The Commutativity: No matter which way you prepare the onion, you end up with the same finely minced result:
  • Path 1: Roughly chop, then fine mince the chunks = g ∘ f
  • Path 2: Fine mince the whole onion directly = h
  • These must be equal: g ∘ f = h

It doesn't matter if you chop roughly first or go straight to mincing—you get the same finely minced onion either way.

Triangle: Two paths from A to C must be equal
The Mathematics:
$$g \circ f = h$$

This says: "Chopping then refining equals fine mincing directly."

Example 2: COMMUTATIVE SQUARE

Recipe Scaling with Unit Conversion

The Story:

You have a chocolate chip cookie recipe. Does it matter if you double the recipe and then convert to metric, vs. converting to metric first and then doubling?

Objects:
  • A = Original recipe (US cups/tbsp)
  • B = Doubled recipe (US cups/tbsp)
  • C = Original recipe (grams/ml)
  • D = Doubled recipe (grams/ml)
The Morphisms:
  • f: Double all ingredients (A → B)
  • g: Convert to metric units (A → C)
  • h: Convert to metric units (B → D)
  • k: Double all ingredients (C → D)
The Commutativity:
  • Path 1: Double the recipe, THEN convert to metric = h ∘ f
  • Path 2: Convert to metric, THEN double = k ∘ g
  • These must be equal: h ∘ f = k ∘ g

Multiplication and unit conversion commute—you get the same final recipe either way!

Commutative Square: Both paths give same result
The Mathematics:
$$h \circ f = k \circ g$$

This says: "Doubling then converting = Converting then doubling."

Example 3: PRODUCT (Universal Construction)

Creating a Dating Profile

The Story:

You're creating an online dating profile. The profile needs two pieces of information combined together.

Objects:
  • A = Set of names {Alice, Bob, Charlie}
  • B = Set of hobbies {Cooking, Reading, Hiking}
  • P = All possible (name, hobby) pairs
The Product:

P = A × B has 3 × 3 = 9 elements:

  • (Alice, Cooking)
  • (Alice, Reading)
  • (Alice, Hiking)
  • (Bob, Cooking)
  • (Bob, Reading)
  • (Bob, Hiking)
  • (Charlie, Cooking)
  • (Charlie, Reading)
  • (Charlie, Hiking)
The Projections:
  • π₁: Extract the name from a profile
  • π₂: Extract the hobby from a profile
The Universal Property:

Imagine you're importing data from another app (X) that knows about people and their hobbies. The product P guarantees that there exists a UNIQUE way to pair them up correctly, preserving all the information from the external source.

Product: Unique pairing of external data
The Mathematics:
$$\pi_1 \circ h = f \quad \text{and} \quad \pi_2 \circ h = g$$

This says: "The unique function h preserves both external data functions."

Example 4: PULLBACK (Fiber Product)

Finding Compatible Couples

The Story:

You're a matchmaker. You have hikers and photographers, and you want to find pairs who like the same locations.

Objects:
  • A = People who love hiking {Alice, Bob, Charlie}
  • B = People interested in photography {Diana, Eugene, Frank}
  • C = Geographic regions {Mountains, Coast, Desert}
  • P = Compatible couples (pullback)
The Maps:
  • k: Where each hiker prefers to hike (A → C)
  • h: Where each photographer prefers to shoot (B → C)
The Pullback:

P = {(person from A, person from B) : they like the same location}

Compatible pairs in P:

  • (Alice, Diana) — both love Mountains
  • (Bob, Eugene) — both love Coast
  • (Charlie, Diana) — both love Mountains
The Interpretation:

The pullback P is the set of compatible pairs—people who would enjoy outdoor activities together in the same location!

Pullback: Finding compatible pairs via intermediary
The Mathematics:
$$k \circ f = h \circ g$$

This says: "Every compatible couple maps to the same region, regardless of path."

Example 5: PUSHOUT (Universal Gluing)

Combining Two Neighborhoods

The Story:

You're merging two neighborhoods along a shared central plaza, respecting all existing connections.

Objects:
  • A = North Neighborhood {Houses N1, N2, N3}
  • B = South Neighborhood {Houses S1, S2, S3}
  • C = Central Plaza {buildings P1, P2}
  • P = The merged city (A ∪_C B)
The Maps:
  • f: Connection points from North to Plaza (A → C)
  • g: Connection points from South to Plaza (B → C)
The Universal Property:

P is the best way to combine the two neighborhoods while respecting that both neighborhoods connect to the central plaza. In the merged city, we identify these common connection points.

Pushout: Universal gluing respecting shared boundaries
The Mathematics:
$$f' \circ f = g' \circ g$$

This says: "Both neighborhoods' connections to the plaza are respected in the merge."

Example 6: EXACT SEQUENCE

A Restaurant Supply Chain

The Story:

Tracking ingredients through a restaurant kitchen from arrival to plating.

Objects:
  • A = Fresh ingredients arriving {tomatoes, lettuce, cheese, ...}
  • B = Prepared dishes in the kitchen {Salad, Pasta, Soup, ...}
  • C = Plated meals ready to serve
The Morphisms:
  • f: Transform fresh ingredients into prepared dishes (A → B)
  • g: Plate the prepared dishes (B → C)
Exactness at B: image(f) = kernel(g)

This means every prepared dish came from fresh ingredients, and dishes that can't be plated are exactly those improperly prepared. Nothing wasted, nothing unexpected!

Exact Sequence: Data flowing without loss
The Mathematics:
$$\text{im}(f) = \ker(g)$$

This says: "Everything prepared gets used, and nothing unexpected appears."

Example 7: NATURAL TRANSFORMATION

Consistent Unit Conversion

The Story:

You're a translator for recipes. Two chefs work in different units, and you translate consistently.

Objects and Functors:
  • F = Recipes in US units (cups, tablespoons)
  • G = Recipes in metric units (ml, grams)
  • η = Your translation function
The Naturality:

If a chef scales a recipe (doubles it), your translation is consistent: scaling first then translating equals translating first then scaling. The translation "respects" the scaling operation!

Natural Transformation: Consistent operations across contexts
The Mathematics:
$$G(f) \circ \eta_A = \eta_B \circ F(f)$$

This says: "Translation commutes with scaling—the translation is natural."

Example 8: ADJOINT FUNCTORS

Free Samples and Discounted Purchases

The Story:

A coffee shop offers free sample cups to get people interested, and sells full drinks at a discount to converts. The free samples and discounted sales are in perfect balance—neither side takes advantage.

Objects:
  • F = The "free sample" functor (offers samples to non-customers)
  • G = The "discount purchase" functor (converts samplers to buyers)
  • X = The set of non-customers
  • Y = The set of customers
The Adjunction:
  • F ⊣ G means: "Free samples are the left adjoint of discount purchases"
  • Giving free samples to non-customers optimally prepares them for the discount offer
  • The discount purchase functor optimally converts samples to real sales
The Balance:

There's a perfect, natural bijection between:

  • Morphisms from X to G(Y): "Non-customer X appreciates discount offer G(Y)"
  • Morphisms from F(X) to Y: "Free sample F(X) converts to purchasing behavior Y"

This bijection is natural—it respects all the underlying relationships between customers and offers.

Adjoint Functors: Perfect balance between two operations
The Mathematics:
$$F \dashv G$$
$$\text{Hom}_{\mathcal{D}}(F(X), Y) \cong \text{Hom}_{\mathcal{C}}(X, G(Y))$$

This says: "Free samples and discounts are adjoint—there's a perfect bijection between appreciation of the offer and conversion to purchase."

Summary: Why These Diagrams Matter

Category diagrams aren't just abstract squiggles—they capture relationships and transformations in the real world:

  • Triangles show that different paths to the same goal are equivalent
  • Squares demonstrate that certain operations commute (order doesn't matter)
  • Products capture combining information optimally
  • Pullbacks find compatible elements via shared properties
  • Pushouts combine objects while respecting their overlaps
  • Exact sequences track data flowing through a system
  • Natural transformations ensure consistency across different contexts
  • Adjoint functors reveal perfect balances and dualities

Once you see categories everywhere, you start understanding the hidden structures in mathematics—and in life!

The Big Picture

Category diagrams are the language of relationships. They tell stories about how things transform, connect, and balance. Master these eight patterns, and you've got a toolkit for understanding structure at the deepest level.
